7 Ways U.S. States are Setting the Stage for Climate Action

Although the American people may appear to be divided, but when it comes to preserving the environment, 25 governors have banded together to support the Paris Agreement on climate change.
The governors of California, Washington, and New York came out in support of the Paris Agreement five years ago on June 1, 2017, when the Trump administration declared its intention to leave the accord. Since that time, the U.S. Climate Alliance has expanded to include 24 states as well as Puerto Rico. Moreover, it keeps on enlarging.

The governors of the alliance, who represent a diverse range of people, places, and economies, are brought together by a shared goal: to demonstrate that states can take the lead on climate action while boosting clean energy economies, enhancing public health, and constructing more resilient communities.
The U.S. Climate Alliance currently represents 60% of the country's GDP, 40% of its greenhouse gas emissions, and 55% of its population. The alliance would have the third-largest economy in the world after the United States and China, if it were a nation. These states have been implementing climate change leadership over the past five years because they understand that it cannot wait.

The U.S. Climate Alliance's member states have taken the following seven significant climate change-related actions:
1. Set challenging targets to cut greenhouse gas emissions. Every state that joins the Alliance commits to enacting laws that advance the Paris Agreement's objectives, which call for at least a 26-28% decrease in greenhouse gas emissions from 2005 levels by 2025, while many are pressing for much greater cuts. For instance, the state of California set a goal for itself to become carbon neutral by 2045. The state of New Mexico set the target of lowering GHG emissions by 45% below 2005 levels by 2030.
2. A law was signed to promote renewable energy. The alliance's member states are advancing the green economy, which is crucial for lowering pollution and generating jobs. A law was established in Nevada requiring it to obtain 50% of its electricity from renewable sources by 2030. In addition, the governor and lieutenant governor of Minnesota, Tim Walz and Peggy Flanagan, unveiled a new set of policy recommendations that will see Minnesota achieve 100% renewable energy in its electrical sector by 2050.
3. Advocated for increased energy effectiveness. Energy efficiency is a key component of any state's climate change strategy because it accounts for 40% of all energy use in homes and commercial buildings in the United States. A groundbreaking norm that will enhance the energy efficiency of thousands of major commercial buildings around the state was established by legislation that Governor Jay Inslee of Washington state signed into law.
4. Policies for Zero Emission Vehicles are being accelerated. The greatest source of emissions in the transportation industry, passenger vehicles, are being reduced at a faster rate than any other state in the alliance. The Colorado Air Quality Control Commission unanimously decided to start the process of requiring automakers to produce 5% of their vehicles for sale in Colorado as electric vehicles by the year 2023 in May 2019. A bill that offers subsidies to those who install new electric vehicle charging stations or improve current ones was passed by the legislature of Hawaii.
5. Regulations to reduce dangerous air pollutants. While carbon dioxide pollution receives the majority of attention, other short-lived climate pollutants including black carbon, methane, and hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) also pose serious threats to the environment and human health. Plans to reduce methane leaks from landfills and natural gas infrastructure were made public by Virginia in 2019. With the intention of preventing federal rollbacks and outlawing the use of dangerous HFCs, Connecticut, Maryland, and New York plan to introduce restrictions in 2019. Washington and Vermont have also recently enacted legislation along these lines.
6. Developed fresh finance options for resilient communities and renewable energy. Governor Charlie Baker of Massachusetts has authorized nearly $2.4 billion in investments to protect people, communities, and businesses from the effects of climate change, as well as to maintain the environment and enhance recreational possibilities. Additionally, Colorado established a new "Green Bank" to use private funding as leverage to encourage investment in clean energy projects.
7. Created unique resources and tools to assist the state in addressing climate change. State governments require specialized tools and resources in order to monitor their climate action efforts and evaluate the risks from consequences. For instance, Governor Roy Cooper of North Carolina published the state's greenhouse gas inventory, which keeps track of current emissions and forecasts future ones. To assist North Carolina's coastal towns in coping with problems brought on by climate impacts, such as rising sea levels, the state also launched a new website called Coastal Adaptation and Resiliency.

The above-listed initiatives only represent a small portion of the state-level climate action taking place. Additionally, regional and local efforts at the county and city levels continue to advance efforts to combat climate change across the United States. In the end, increased action from everyone, everywhere, and at all levels is necessary to address the global climate crisis. "Climate change is a race we can win, but we must scale up ambition now," said United Nations Secretary-General António Guterres.
